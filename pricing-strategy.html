100Lights · Internal Strategy
Early Access Pricing & Growth Plan
Prepared July 2026 Brae Campbell, Founder Confidential
Overview

100Lights launches with a deliberately low early-access price designed to do two things simultaneously: attract the first paying users needed to break even quickly, and create a motivated group of testers who give real feedback in exchange for real benefits. As the product matures, pricing rises for new users only — early adopters keep their rate for life.

This document captures the pricing structure, milestone triggers, feedback reward program, and the principles behind each decision.

Price Increase Milestones

Prices for new users rise at specific user-count milestones. Internally, these are triggered by user count. Externally, they are communicated as reflecting a more capable, stable product — not simply demand pricing.

Launch → 100 users
Early Access pricing: $5/module/mo
Goal is beta testers and feedback, not revenue maximization. Every user who signs up here is grandfathered at $5 for life.
100 → 500 users
New user price rises to $8/module/mo
Message to new users: "We've shipped stems, export, mobile support, and the product is substantially more complete." Early users keep $5 permanently.
500 → 2,000 users
New user price rises to $12/module/mo (evaluate based on feature set at the time)
By this point the product should be shipping near-daily and the price should feel self-evident. Offer annual billing prominently to lock in users at the current rate.
2,000+ users
Full market pricing — evaluate quarterly
Comparables: Ableton (one-time $400), Descript ($24/mo), Figma ($12/mo). At this scale, $15–20/module/mo is defensible for a full DAW.
Key rule: Never raise prices on existing subscribers without at least 90 days' notice and a clear explanation tied to product capability, not business metrics. Grandfathered users should never see a price increase — if a subscription lapses and they re-subscribe, they re-enter at the current rate.
Feedback Reward Program

Early users who actively contribute to improving the product receive concrete rewards. Criteria are specific and verifiable — "good feedback" is never used as a subjective bar, because that creates resentment.

Early access + recognition
Why this works: Free months are time-limited and cost you very little (COGS per free user ≈ $0.18/mo). Beta access costs nothing and turns your most engaged users into advocates. Referrals reduce your customer acquisition cost to zero for that cohort.
Annual Billing

Offer annual billing from day one alongside monthly. Early access annual rate: $50/module/year (equivalent to $4.17/mo — a ~17% discount). This should be presented as the default option on the pricing page.

1
Cash upfront: Annual payers give you 12 months of revenue on day one — critical for covering fixed costs during the growth phase.
2
Dramatically lower churn: Monthly subscribers cancel during slow periods; annual subscribers forget they're subscribed and keep renewing. Expect 3–4× better retention on annual plans.
3
Locks in the early-access price for a full year: Annual payers feel like they're getting a deal and securing their rate. This is a genuine benefit, not a manufactured one.
Guiding Principles
·
Grandfathering is permanent and unconditional. If an early user's subscription lapses — even briefly — they re-enter at the current public price. Make this clear upfront so it isn't a surprise later.
·
Price increases are tied to capability, not demand. Every public price increase should accompany a changelog. Users should be able to look at what shipped and feel the new price is fair, even if they personally don't benefit from the new features.
·
Give 90 days' notice before any price change. For grandfathered users this is moot. For others, adequate notice is both respectful and strategically smart — it often triggers a wave of annual upgrades from users who want to lock in the current rate.
·
The goal of early access is great feedback, not revenue. Revenue is a side effect. Every product decision during this phase should be filtered through: "does this help us learn what to build next?"
