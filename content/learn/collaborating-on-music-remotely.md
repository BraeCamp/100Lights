# How to Actually Collaborate on a Track Remotely

Ask anyone who's done a lot of remote work why a collaboration died and they'll tell you it was scheduling, or that the other person got busy, or that it just lost momentum. That's the polite version. The real version, almost every time, is one of two things: nobody knew who was allowed to change the arrangement, or somebody worked for three hours on the wrong file.

Nobody says this out loud because both sound petty. Neither is petty. They're the entire failure mode, and once you know that, remote collaboration turns out to be mostly a paperwork problem with a music-shaped hat on.

Here's how it actually gets run.

## The version problem, and why it's not about backups

You know the classic shape. `track_v3_FINAL_JAKE.wav`. `track_v3_FINAL_JAKE_v2.wav`. The bounce your collaborator wrote to at 2am, which was already two edits behind. Then the awkward conversation where somebody's work has to be redone, and it's never redone, and the project quietly ends there.

The instinct is to fix this with discipline — naming conventions, a shared drive, a message every time you upload. It doesn't hold. Not because people are careless, but because the moment two people are working in different time zones, "the current version" stops being a fact and becomes a guess. Every file-passing workflow degrades into guessing. It just takes longer with better naming.

The fix isn't a better convention. It's removing the file from the middle. A shared project link in [100Lights](https://100lights.com) points at *the* project, not a copy of it — you both open the same session, and the question of which version is current stops existing. Live co-editing means you can watch each other work in real time, or work in it hours apart, and either way there is only one thing to be inside.

This sounds like a convenience feature. It's not. It's the thing that decides whether the collaboration survives week three.

## The ownership problem, which is worse

Now the part actually nobody documents.

Two competent people in one session will, within about forty minutes, start quietly undoing each other. Not out of ego. Out of taste. You pull the hats back because they're busy; they push them up because they carry the groove; neither of you mentions it; you both do it twice more. Nothing is said. The session gets tense in a way that gets blamed on the music.

There are two layers to solving this, and 100Lights now hands you the first one directly. In the Share panel, everyone you add gets a role you can change at any moment — **Can view** (listen and follow along, free), **Can edit** (co-edit live), or **Owner** (that plus managing who else is in). Anyone who should be reacting rather than rearranging — the friend whose ear you trust, the singer who's finished tracking — goes to *Can view*: they hear every change live and leave comments, but they can't move a section by accident. That removes most of the quiet undoing on its own, because most of it comes from people having edit access they never actually needed. (Live editing is a Pro feature on the editor's account; viewing and commenting are always free.)

The second layer is a convention, and it's for the people who *do* have edit access: **one person owns the arrangement.** Not the song. The arrangement — section order, lengths, what enters and what drops. Among the editors, that person is the only one who moves, deletes, or duplicates sections. Everyone else works *inside* the sections they're given.

That's the trade knowledge. It's the same reason a studio has one person at the desk while four people have opinions, and it's why professional sessions with six people in the room somehow move faster than your two-person collaboration.

Assign the arrangement owner in the first ten minutes, out loud, before anyone's precious about anything — and set everyone who isn't editing to *Can view* so the agreement has teeth. It can rotate between songs. It cannot rotate mid-session.

The rest divides cleanly. Whoever wrote the chords owns harmony. Whoever's singing owns the vocal and every decision about it, including the takes you preferred. Sound design and mix are usually one person, and if they're two, they need to agree on which of them touches the sends and returns, because two people on a reverb bus is its own special disaster.

## How to give notes without ending a friendship

The second unwritten skill, and the one that separates people who get called back.

Give notes at a **timestamp**, about a **feeling**, and never about a fix.

"The pre-chorus feels like it's waiting for something" is a good note. "Put a riser at bar 24" is not a note — it's you doing their job from a distance, and it lands as a correction whether or not you meant it that way. If you have a fix in mind, say the feeling first and offer the fix second, explicitly flagged as one option. The difference in how those two land is out of all proportion to the wording.

Timeline comments exist for exactly this: you pin the note to the spot in the arrangement it refers to, and it stops being a critique of the track and becomes a mark on a specific bar. Something about the note being *attached to a place* takes almost all the heat out of it. Use session chat for the live back-and-forth while you're both in there, and leave the comments for anything that has to survive until tomorrow.

Three habits that carry more weight than any of the above:

- Lead with what's working, and be specific about it — "the second half of the bass line" tells them more than "the bass is great," and it tells them what not to touch.
- Cap your notes. Five per pass. If you have nineteen, you have a structural problem, and you should say the structural thing instead of nineteen symptoms.
- Never re-give a note. If you said it and they didn't take it, they heard you and disagreed. Let it go or let the track go.

## The setup that actually works

Concretely, for a two-person track across time zones:

Start the session together, live, for the first hour. This is non-negotiable and people skip it constantly. That hour sets the tempo, the key, the reference track, and the arrangement owner — and if you skip it you will spend three weeks discovering you had different songs in mind.

Then work asynchronously, in the same project, leaving timeline comments where you've changed something the other person might care about. Both of you export a WAV at the end of every session, and keep them — not as backups, but so you can hear the week's direction as a sequence. If someone needs to work outside the session, export stems as a zip, but treat that as a one-way trip: what comes back is a new audio part, not a replacement project.

Set an end date at the start. Say it out loud in the first hour. Collaborations don't die from disagreement, they die from an unbounded middle where neither person wants to be the one who says it's done.

@ear Play the arrangement owner's latest bounce and the version from a week earlier back to back. Don't judge them — count the differences you can identify without looking at the timeline. If you can't name three, the project has been busy rather than moving.

## Finding the other person

The hard part is not the workflow. It's that most people don't have anyone to do this with.

Start by making things in public. Publish samples, presets, or a finished song to the [100Lights community](https://100lights.com/community) and pay attention to whose sounds you keep pulling into your own projects — that's a better compatibility signal than any conversation. Reach out to someone whose work you've actually used. Propose one song, one deadline, one arrangement owner.

The shared session, the comments, the stems all run free in the browser — live co-editing is the one piece that asks for a Pro plan on the editor's side. Either way the cost of trying a collaboration with a stranger is a link. The reason it usually still fails has never been the software.
