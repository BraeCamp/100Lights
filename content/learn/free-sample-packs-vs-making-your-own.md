# Stop Downloading Sample Packs

Your drums don't sound generic because you picked the wrong pack. They sound generic because forty thousand other people downloaded the same one, and roughly none of you changed anything about it.

This is the part nobody wants to hear, because downloading packs feels like progress. It has all the shape of work — you browse, you evaluate, you organize, you rename folders. At the end you have more than you started with. But a hard drive full of somebody else's decisions is not a sound. It's a queue.

I spent about three years collecting. I had a folder called `DRUMS_FINAL` and another called `DRUMS_FINAL_2` and I could not have told you the difference between them. What broke the habit wasn't discipline. It was a broken cupboard door in a rented flat that made a sound I couldn't find in any of it.

## The homogenization is measurable, and you can hear it

Go listen to twenty tracks tagged "lo-fi hip hop" or "future bass" uploaded this month. Not critically — just listen to the transients. You will hear the same clap. Not a similar clap. The same one, from the same free pack that got passed around, sitting at the same spot in the stereo field because nobody moved it.

This is what happens when the raw material is centralized. Every producer in 2004 was limited by what they physically owned — a busted keyboard, a CD, a tape their uncle left behind — and that limitation is exactly why records from that era sound like specific places. Scarcity forced fingerprints. Abundance erased them.

The industry sells you the opposite story, because the industry sells packs. "Pro-grade samples, mixed and mastered, ready to drop in." Read that last part again. *Ready to drop in* means *pre-decided*. You're buying a sound that has already been EQ'd for a track that isn't yours.

@theory A sample that arrives already compressed, already brightened, already sitting in its own little pocket of reverb has been shaped to sound finished in isolation. That's the problem. Nothing in a good mix sounds finished in isolation — it sounds finished in company. Pre-polished samples fight each other for the same space because they were each built to win alone.

## Record one object. Today. Badly.

Here's the assignment, and it takes eleven minutes.

Find something in the room that makes a noise you like. A cupboard door. A ceramic mug struck with a spoon. A stapler. The hollow thunk of your desk when you knuckle it. A lighter wheel. The chain on a lamp. Your own chest, hit with a flat palm.

Arm a track in [100Lights](https://100lights.com), set a count-in, and record it. Record it twelve times, not once — you want variations, because twelve slightly different mug hits layered across a hi-hat line is the entire reason live drums sound alive and programmed drums often don't.

Your phone mic is fine. Your laptop mic is fine. The recording will be quiet, roomy, and a little hissy, and every one of those flaws is a fingerprint nobody else has.

@video Recording twelve strikes of a ceramic mug into a single track, then slicing them into a usable hi-hat layer

Then mangle it. Pitch it down four octaves and a stapler becomes a kick with a wooden knock in its tail that no drum machine makes. Reverse it. Put it through a short delay and only keep the delay. Compress it until the room noise swells up between hits and becomes part of the groove. Run it through the EQ, hard high-pass at first so all you have is the click, then decide what you actually want back.

The word for this is not *sound design*. It's just *deciding*. Every one of those choices is a decision a pack author made for you and charged you nothing for, which should have been your first clue.

@math If you want a sample to land an octave lower, halve its playback rate — 2x length, half the frequency. Four octaves down is a rate of 1/16, which stretches a 0.2-second stapler click into a 3.2-second groan. Pitching *up* a perfect fifth is a rate of 1.5. None of this matters if you'd rather just drag the pitch control until it sounds right, which is what most people do and what I do.

## So what's the 1000+ sound library for, then

Fair question, since 100Lights ships one.

Use it as a starting point, never as a destination. There's a real difference between "I need a kick to hold this idea together while I work out the bassline" and "this kick is the kick." The first is scaffolding. The second is surrender.

The practical workflow that actually holds up: build the loop fast with library sounds so you can hear whether the idea is any good, then go back and replace the two or three sounds that carry the track's personality. Usually that's the hat, the top-end percussion, and whatever weird thing sits in the background. Leave the kick. Nobody has ever identified a producer by their kick drum, and you will waste a week trying.

@ear Load any library snare and loop a bar. Now layer one recording of you clapping once, badly, off-mic, at maybe 40% volume underneath it. Don't tune it. Don't time-align it perfectly. Play both. The snare stops sounding like a file and starts sounding like a thing that happened in a room — and the reason is the imperfection you were about to fix.

## The counter-argument, which is real

Somebody's going to say: professional records use commercial packs constantly. True. Splice is all over the charts. I'm not going to pretend otherwise.

But listen to what those producers do with them. A charting record might use a pack sample as one of nine layers in a snare, buried at -18 dB, doing nothing but adding a specific frequency around 4 kHz that the recorded layers lacked. That's a pack sample used as an ingredient. What most bedroom producers do is use it as a meal.

The difference isn't access. It's that one person is treating the sample as raw material and the other is treating it as a finished product. Same file. Completely different relationship to it.

## What actually happens when you switch

The first month is worse. I want to be honest about that. Your recorded hi-hats will sound thin next to the mastered ones you're used to, your levels will be inconsistent, and you'll be tempted to go back. Push through it — what you're hearing is the absence of somebody else's mixing, and you have to learn to supply it yourself.

The second thing that happens is that you get faster. Not slower, which is what everyone expects. Browsing a 40GB library for the right clap costs twenty minutes and ends in a compromise. Hitting a mug and recording it costs four and ends with exactly the sound you imagined, because you made the sound match the idea instead of hunting for an idea that matched an available sound.

And the third thing: people start asking what you used. That question is the whole point. Nobody asks what pack you used when they already know.

When you land on something you like, publish it — the [community](https://100lights.com/community) is full of samples, presets, and recipes people made themselves, and one weird recording of a radiator being hit with a wrench is worth more to another producer than another folder of 808s. Browse it for oddities, not for completeness.

Everything above runs free in the browser: the mic recording, the pitch mangling, the effect chains, the library you're raiding for scaffolding. No downloads, which is a little funny given the topic.

Go hit something and record it.
