# Recording Vocals at Home With Just a Browser

You have been told that you cannot record a usable vocal until you own a large-diaphragm condenser, an audio interface with clean preamps, and a room with foam on the walls. That advice has cost more aspiring singers more years than any other lie in this hobby. It is also the reason you have a $180 microphone in a box and no finished songs.

Here is the actual ranking, and I will defend it: the performance matters most, the gain staging second, the room third, and the microphone a distant fourth. Nobody who has ever loved a song loved it because of the capsule. Billie Eilish's first record was tracked in a bedroom into a mic that costs less than a pair of shoes, and it won Album of the Year. The people telling you to upgrade are selling microphones.

So let's do the opposite of what the tutorials say. Open [100Lights](https://100lights.com) in a browser, plug in whatever mic you already own — including the one in your laptop, I mean it — and record a vocal today.

## The room is the mic

Every microphone in your price range hears the same thing: you, plus the reflections of you bouncing off six flat surfaces. The expensive mic hears those reflections in higher fidelity. That is not an improvement.

A small hard room adds a dense smear of reflections arriving a few milliseconds after your voice. Your ear reads that smear as "recorded in a bathroom," and no EQ removes it, because the reflections and the voice are the same signal.

@theory Reverb is only pleasant when it arrives late enough to sound like a separate event. When the reflection lands almost on top of the direct sound, your ear doesn't hear space at all — it hears a hollowness, a boxy ring on certain notes, as though the voice were spoken into a cupboard. That's why bedroom vocals sound small even when they're loud.

The fix costs nothing. Stand in the middle of the room, not against a wall. Get soft things around you: an open closet full of hanging coats is the best vocal booth most people own, and it beats a hundred dollars of foam squares. Kill the fan. Kill the fridge if you can reach the plug.

Then sing closer to the mic than feels natural. Proximity means the direct sound is louder relative to the room, and the ratio between those two is the entire ballgame.

## Gain staging, which is the boring part that actually matters

Arm your track and watch the meter while you sing the loudest phrase in the song — not a test "hello," the actual belt. You want the peaks landing well short of the top. If the meter kisses the ceiling even once, the take is over; digital clipping is not warm, it is a crackle you cannot remove.

The instinct is to record hot because quiet takes feel weak. Resist it. A quiet, clean take can be turned up later with no penalty. A clipped take is garbage forever.

@math A 24-bit recording gives you roughly 144 dB of theoretical dynamic range. Peaking at -12 dBFS instead of -1 dBFS costs eleven of them and leaves about 133 — far more than your room's noise floor will ever let you use. The headroom is free; the clipping is not.

Set it, sing the loudest line twice, adjust, then stop fiddling.

## Turn on the count-in, then stop counting

100Lights gives you a count-in before recording starts. Use it every time. Four clicks before the punch does something no amount of practice does: it puts the tempo into your body before your first syllable, which is why the first line of your take stops arriving late.

Singers who skip the count-in almost always drift flat in pitch on the opening word too. You're guessing at the pitch and the placement simultaneously. Take one of those jobs away.

## Monitoring: sing into the effects, not into the dry truth

This is the single change that makes the biggest difference to how people sing, and almost no beginner does it.

When you monitor your own voice completely dry — raw, naked, no processing — you hear every flaw magnified, and you sing carefully. Careful is the enemy. Careful is thin, hesitant, pitch-perfect, and dead.

100Lights supports input monitoring with effects, meaning the reverb, delay, and compressor on your track are audible in your headphones while you record. Put a plate reverb and a slap delay on the vocal chain before you press record. Not for the recording — the recording stays dry — for you.

@ear Record eight bars of a chorus with dry monitoring. Then, without changing anything else, add a medium reverb and a compressor to the track's chain, turn on input monitoring, and sing the same eight bars again. Compare the two takes with all the effects muted. Listen for how much harder you pushed on the second one, and how much less you flinched off the ends of phrases.

Every professional vocal booth has done this since the 1960s. It is not cheating. It is how you get a singer to sing.

## Loop takes, and the third one

Set a loop over the section and let it record around and around. You get take after take stacked without touching a single button between them, which matters more psychologically than technically — the moment you stop and evaluate, you start protecting yourself.

The first take is you finding the melody. The second is you being careful. The third is usually the one. Somewhere around the sixth you get tired and something honest falls out; keep that one too, because tired singers stop performing and start meaning it.

Then comp. Pull the best line from take three, the best word from take six, and you have a vocal nobody could sing in one pass. Every record you own was made this way. Yes, that one too.

## Latency, and why your take sounds early

If you've ever recorded a vocal that felt perfectly in time while you sang it and landed noticeably ahead of the beat on playback, you have not lost your sense of rhythm. Your computer took a few milliseconds to get your voice from the mic to the disk, and it stamped the audio where it arrived, not where you sang it.

100Lights does latency compensation automatically, shifting the take back by the measured round-trip delay so it lands where you actually performed it. Leave it on and forget it. If a take still feels a hair off against the drums, nudge the whole clip — that's a musical decision now, not a technical one.

@theory Vocals that sit a fraction behind the beat feel relaxed and soulful; ones that sit a fraction ahead feel urgent and pushy. Neither is wrong. Rap and gospel are built on this. But you want that to be a choice you made, not an artifact of your hardware.

## The part where you actually do it

Here's the whole workflow, and it's shorter than the argument that preceded it. Open a project. Add a track and arm it. Set a count-in of four. Add reverb and a compressor, enable input monitoring, and put on closed-back headphones so the backing track doesn't bleed into the mic. Set a loop over your verse. Sing it six times without stopping. Comp the best moments together.

That is a real vocal session, and everything in it — the recording, the effects, the comping, the WAV export at the end — runs in a browser tab, free, with no plugins to install.

You will hate the first one. Everyone hates the first one, and the people who quit at that stage were never going to hear their own voice charitably no matter what mic they bought. Do six songs. Compare song six to song one. That difference is worth more than any purchase.

When you've got something you don't hate, publish it to the [100Lights community](https://100lights.com/community) and go listen to what other people made in their closets. It's a useful calibration: half of them sound better than you expect, and none of them were recorded in a treated room.
