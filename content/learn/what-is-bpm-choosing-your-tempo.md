# What Is BPM, and How Do You Pick a Tempo?

Nobody's slow song is slow because they picked a low number. Stop treating tempo like a setting you get right once at the start and then forget. That habit — pick 120, never look again — is why so many beginner tracks feel like they're running on a treadmill: technically moving, going nowhere.

BPM stands for beats per minute. It's the count of the steady pulse underneath a song — the thing you'd tap on a table, the thing a metronome clicks. A hundred and twenty BPM means a hundred and twenty of those pulses every minute, which works out to two per second. That's the whole definition. It is not complicated, and the internet has spent a decade making it sound complicated so it can sell you a course.

Here's the part the tutorials skip: the number itself is almost meaningless. What matters is what the number does to a listener's body.

## Tempo is a feeling before it's a number

Play something at sixty and the ear reads it as space, patience, weight. Every event has room around it. You notice the decay of a piano note because nothing arrives to cover it. Push the same notes to a hundred and forty and the ear stops hearing individual events and starts hearing motion — the song becomes a thing that carries you rather than a thing you observe.

@theory The reason isn't mystical. Below roughly a beat per second, the pulse sits near the pace of a calm heartbeat, so the body reads it as rest. As the tempo climbs past the low hundreds, the beat overtakes a resting pulse and starts to feel like exertion — walking, then jogging, then sprinting. That physical mapping is why fast music feels urgent and slow music feels heavy, and it's older than any genre you could name.

This is the whole reason tempo matters, and it's why "just pick 120" is bad advice. You're not choosing a number. You're choosing how hard you want to make a stranger's chest work.

@ear Take any loop you've got. Play it at your current tempo, then drop the tempo by forty and play it again, then raise it forty above the original and play it a third time. Don't change a single note. Same melody, same drums. Listen to how the emotion changes underneath your hands — how the sad version and the frantic version are the same file. That gap is the thing you actually control.

## The real ranges, by genre

People want a table. Fine, but read the warning first: these are gravity wells, not laws. Genres cluster around certain tempos because the feel of that tempo is half of what makes the genre. Break the range on purpose and you can, but know that you're leaving.

Ballads and slow soul live low — roughly sixty to eighty. Adele's "Someone Like You" sits near sixty-seven, and it feels like it because the space between piano chords is doing half the emotional work.

Classic hip-hop — boom bap — clusters around eighty-five to ninety-five. Dr. Dre's "Nuthin' but a 'G' Thang" lands around ninety-three. That pocket exists because it's the tempo a head naturally nods at; go much faster and the swagger falls out of it.

Most pop and rock breathe between about a hundred and a hundred and thirty. "Billie Jean" runs near a hundred and seventeen, which is fast enough to move and slow enough to strut.

House and a lot of mainstream dance music sit tight between a hundred and twenty and a hundred and twenty-eight, because that's the range a DJ can beatmatch all night without wrecking anyone's legs. Techno pushes a little past it. Disco and a good chunk of funk hang a bit lower, in the hundred-teens to low twenties.

Drum and bass is the outlier everyone points to — a hundred and seventy to a hundred and seventy-five, almost without exception. It sounds twice as fast as it is, because the kick and snare pattern often implies a slow half-time groove riding on top of those frantic hats.

@math Want the exact ranges converted to something you can feel? One beat lasts 60000 divided by the BPM, in milliseconds. So 120 BPM is 60000 / 120 = 500 ms per beat — dead on half a second. Boom bap at 93 is about 645 ms. Drum and bass at 174 is roughly 345 ms per beat. A ballad at 67 stretches each beat out to about 896 ms, nearly a full second of air per pulse.

Notice something in those numbers: drum and bass at a hundred and seventy-four and boom bap at eighty-seven are exactly double. Halve a DnB tempo and you're in hip-hop territory. That's not a coincidence, and it's the trick behind half-time — one genre wearing another's clothes.

## So how do you actually pick one?

Don't pick from the table. Pick from the reference.

Whatever song made you want to write this one — the mood you're chasing — go find its tempo and start there. You can tap it in yourself. Every DAW worth using, this one included, has a tempo field and a TAP button sitting right at the top of the transport bar. Load the reference track, or just play it on your phone, and tap the button in time with the beat. After a few taps you'll have a number within a couple of BPM of the real thing. Start there, then move.

@tip Don't trust the first tap reading blindly — tap for a full eight beats or so, not two. A short tap can land on double or half the real tempo, especially in music with a strong offbeat. If the number looks twice as fast as the song feels, it probably is. Halve it.

Once you've got a starting number, the honest move is to write a bar or two and then nudge the tempo up and down by five while it loops. You'll feel the right one lock in. It stops sounding like a demo and starts sounding like the thing in your head. That's not a metaphor — it's a specific, findable moment, and it's usually not the round number you assumed.

## Changing it later is not cheating

Here's the belief I actually want to kill: that the tempo is a foundation you pour once and build on top of, so changing it later means tearing the house down.

Wrong. The tempo field is just a number you can edit whenever you want, and every clip you've already made will stretch or snap to follow it — your drums stay in time, your melody stays in time, the whole project simply moves to the new pulse. Wrote your verse at a hundred and twenty and realized the chorus needs to hit harder? Try the whole song at a hundred and twenty-four. Costs you ten seconds to find out.

I've thrown away a lot of loops that weren't bad loops. They were good loops at the wrong tempo, and I couldn't hear it for months because I'd decided the tempo early and stopped questioning it. That's the real failure mode — not picking wrong, but refusing to revisit. The number at the top of the transport is the single most powerful control in the whole program, and most people set it once and never touch it again.

@try Take a finished loop you're bored of. Don't change a note. Move the tempo by twelve in either direction and sit with it for a minute. Half the time you'll rediscover the loop.

If you want a fast way to calibrate your ear, pull up finished tracks other people have shared in the [100Lights community](https://100lights.com/community), check what tempo they built at, and play a few back to back. You'll start to hear the difference between ninety and a hundred and forty the way you hear the difference between walking and running — instantly, in your body, before you ever read the number.

The metronome isn't the boss of the song. You are. The number just tells you how fast the room is breathing.
