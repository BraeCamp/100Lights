# Writing Melodies Without Touching a Piano

Don't read ahead. Open a project in [100Lights](https://100lights.com), add a track with a piano or a soft synth on it, and open the piano roll. Set the tempo to something slow — 90 or so. You'll need about twenty minutes and no musical training whatsoever.

I'm not going to tell you what makes a melody work. You're going to find out, and then I'll name it.

## Experiment one: one note, five times

Draw a single note. Any pitch, roughly in the middle of the vertical range. Make it one beat long. Copy it four more times so you have five identical notes in a row, one per beat, filling a bar and a quarter.

Play it — then in the studio drag one and hear what a single move does.

@ear Play the five notes. Then play them again and stay with them long enough to get uncomfortable. Ask yourself one question: does this want to go up, or does it want to go down? Don't reason about it. Just notice which direction your body expects.

Most people feel a pull. Some feel it upward, some down, and if you feel nothing at all, that's information too — the note is sitting there refusing to be a melody, and you're about to learn why.

## Experiment two: move the fifth one

Take that last note and drag it up one row. One. The smallest move the grid allows.

@ear Play all five again. Listen specifically to what happens at the end. Then drag that last note back down to where it started, play it, and drag it up again. Toggle it four or five times so you're comparing the same two seconds over and over.

Now drag it up seven rows instead of one, and play it.

Then drag it *down* three rows from the original, and play it.

You have just heard three different endings to the same phrase. One of them probably felt like the phrase leaned forward. One of them felt like a leap, like the melody suddenly meant something. One of them probably felt like settling, or sagging.

Write down, in your own words, which was which. Do it before you keep reading — the words you choose are the thing you're actually learning.

@theory What you just discovered has a shape to it. A small move feels like continuation: the ear barely registers it as a departure, so the phrase keeps its momentum and stays singable. A large move feels like an event — the ear notices the gap and reads it as intent, which is why the big jumps in songs almost always land on the emotional word. And direction carries weight independent of size. Going up reads as effort, reaching, question. Going down reads as release, arrival, or resignation. This is not a convention someone agreed to. It maps onto what your voice physically does when you're excited versus when you're finished talking.

@math In the piano roll each row is one semitone, and each note has a MIDI number. Middle C is 60. Moving up one row is +1; moving up seven rows is +7, the interval called a perfect fifth, which is the jump at the start of "Twinkle Twinkle Little Star." Moving down three is -3, a minor third — the sighing interval at the end of a hundred thousand blues phrases. You can build every melody in this article without ever knowing these numbers.

## Experiment three: the same phrase, twice

Delete everything. Draw four notes going gently upward — one row, then two rows, then one row, small moves only. One beat each.

Now copy that whole four-note group and paste it directly after itself, so it plays twice in a row.

@ear Play the eight notes. Notice how much more like a melody it feels than the first thing you drew — and notice, honestly, at what point it starts to feel dumb. Somewhere in the second repetition your interest drops off a cliff.

Now select only the second copy and drag the whole thing up two rows, keeping its internal shape identical. Play it again.

That version is doing something the first wasn't. The pattern is recognizable, so your ear isn't lost, but it isn't identical, so your ear isn't bored. Almost every hook you can hum is built on this move — the "Seven Nation Army" riff is the same shape, moved.

## Experiment four: break one note

Keep that eight-note phrase. Take the very last note and drag it upward a long way — six or seven rows above everything else. Play it.

Then take that same note and stretch it so it lasts four beats instead of one. Play it again.

@ear Compare the phrase with a short high ending against the phrase with a long high ending. One of them sounds like a mistake. The other sounds like the end of a chorus. The only difference is duration.

@theory A leap creates tension because the ear expects continuation and got a gap instead. Tension has to resolve into something — either the melody comes back down, which is why leaps in folk songs are usually followed by a walk back, or the note simply lasts long enough to become the destination rather than an interruption. Length is what converts a wrong note into a decision. Hold anything long enough and confidently enough and the ear accepts it as intentional.

## Experiment five: take notes away

Your eight notes are all the same length, evenly spaced, which is why they still sound like an exercise rather than music.

Delete the third note. Delete the sixth. Play what's left.

@ear Listen to the two holes. That silence isn't absence — it's phrasing. Melodies breathe where singers breathe, and a melody with no gaps is a melody nobody can sing.

Then take the note right before each hole and stretch it to fill half the gap. Play again. That's a different feeling entirely: instead of two silences you now have two long notes, and the phrase reads as lyrical rather than clipped.

Neither is better. But you can now produce both on purpose, which you couldn't twenty minutes ago.

## Experiment six: the wrong notes

Here's the one that surprises people. Take your phrase and drag one middle note up or down by a single row — pick a note that's currently sounding fine and deliberately spoil it.

@ear Play it. It'll probably sound sour. Now, without moving that note back, shorten it to a quarter of its length and place it right before a longer note. Play again.

The sourness mostly evaporates. Passing quickly through a note that doesn't belong is not an error — it's an ornament, and jazz, blues, and basically all of Motown are built on notes that would be "wrong" if you held them and are delicious because you don't.

@theory Your ear judges a note largely by how long it commits to it. Brief dissonance reads as movement and colour; sustained dissonance reads as a mistake. This is why the same pitch can be a bad note or the best note in the bar depending only on what surrounds it and how long it stays.

## What you actually learned

Not chords. Not scales. Not one term you'd need to look up. You learned that small moves feel smooth, big moves feel dramatic, repetition with one change is the engine of every hook, length turns accidents into intentions, and silence is a note.

You can now write melodies by drawing rectangles and listening, which is what a lot of professional writers do — plenty of people who play beautifully still write in the grid, because the grid lets you edit a decision instead of re-performing it.

All six experiments run in a free browser tab. When one of them turns into something you like, keep going: drag a chord recipe underneath your melody and hear how the same notes change meaning against different harmony, or export the MIDI and hand it to a friend. And when you've written something you'd defend, publish it to [the 100Lights community](https://100lights.com/community), where you can also pull apart other people's songs note by note — which is experiment seven, and the one that never stops paying.
