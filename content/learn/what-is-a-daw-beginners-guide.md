# What Is a DAW, Really?

Nobody learns a DAW. That whole framing is wrong, and it has stopped more people from making music than lack of talent ever has.

You've seen the videos: eleven hours of "complete beginner course," a screen packed with meters and routing matrices, a man explaining sidechain compression to someone who has not yet made a sound. The implication is that the software is a subject, like organic chemistry, and that you must pass it before you're allowed to write anything. So you watch four hours, absorb nothing, open the program, stare at the grey grid, and close it.

The software is not the subject. Music is the subject. A DAW — digital audio workstation, a name invented by people selling rackmount gear in 1985 — is four things, and you can start using all four in the next ten minutes.

## Thing one: a timeline

Left is earlier. Right is later. Each horizontal lane is one instrument or one recording.

That's the whole idea. It's a spreadsheet where the columns are time. Everything else in the arrangement view — the snapping, the markers, the automation lanes, the fades — is a refinement on "left is earlier."

If you've ever dragged a clip in a video editor, you already know how to use it. Drag a drum loop into a lane. Drag a copy of it to the right so it plays twice. Congratulations: that's arranging, and every record you love is that operation performed a few hundred times with taste.

@ear Take any four-bar loop you have. Copy it out to sixteen bars so it just repeats four times. Play the whole thing and pay attention to exactly where you get bored. Note the bar number. Almost everyone lands somewhere between bar 8 and bar 12 — and that number, not any software feature, is the actual thing you're learning.

Most DAWs, including [100Lights](https://100lights.com), also give you a second way to look at the same material: a Session view, which is a grid of loops you trigger live instead of a timeline you build left to right. It's excellent, it's how a lot of electronic music gets sketched, and you can ignore it entirely until the timeline stops being enough. It'll still be there.

## Thing two: a piano roll

The timeline holds clips. The piano roll is where you decide what's inside them.

Vertical is pitch — higher on the screen means higher in sound. Horizontal is still time. Notes are rectangles. Drag a rectangle up, the note gets higher. Make the rectangle longer, the note lasts longer. Move it right, it happens later.

That's it. That is the entire notation system, and it took the music world four hundred years and a computer to arrive at something this obvious. You do not need to read sheet music. You do not need to know what a key signature is. You need to know that up is up.

@theory Notes that sit close together vertically feel smooth and connected — the ear follows them without effort, which is why singable melodies mostly move in small steps. Notes that jump a long way vertically feel dramatic and deliberate, because the ear registers the gap as an event. Everything people call "melodic writing" lives in the ratio between those two moves.

For drums, the same panel switches into a STEP grid: a row per drum sound, a column per sixteenth note, click to place a hit. Kick on the beats, snare on 2 and 4, hats on the offbeats. You can write a decent drum pattern before you've learned a single term.

## Thing three: a mixer

Every track feeds into a mixer. A mixer does two things you care about and about nine you don't.

The two: volume, and left-right position. Turn things up, turn things down, push the hi-hats slightly to one side so they stop fighting the vocal. That's mixing. Ninety percent of the improvement in a beginner's mixes comes from pulling faders down, and specifically from pulling everything down that isn't the kick, the bass, and the lead.

The nine you don't yet: EQ, compression, reverb, delay, sends, returns, and the rest of the effect chain. These are real and they matter and you should touch them roughly a month from now, when you've noticed a specific problem — "the vocal disappears when the chorus comes in" — and you want a specific tool.

Here is my actual heresy: adding a compressor to a track you don't have a complaint about makes it worse. Every time. Effects are answers, and if you haven't got a question yet, you're just adding noise with a nice interface.

The one exception is reverb, because reverb isn't a fix, it's a place. Send a couple of tracks to a shared reverb return and suddenly they sound like they're in the same room instead of stapled together. That's worth learning early.

## Thing four: an exporter

The button that turns your project into a file other humans can play.

It sounds trivial. It is not — a project file is worthless outside the software that made it, and the number of producers with two hundred unfinished projects and zero exported songs is genuinely tragic. Export early. Export bad versions. A finished bad song teaches you more than an unfinished good one, and it isn't close.

@math WAV at 44.1 kHz is the CD and streaming standard; 48 kHz is the video standard, because film runs at frame rates that divide into it cleanly. Stereo 24-bit WAV runs about 15 MB per minute at 44.1 kHz, so a three-minute song is roughly 45 MB before any compression.

Stems — one file per track, exported as a zip — matter the moment you want someone else to mix your song, or you want to rebuild it somewhere else. MIDI export is the notes without the sounds, which is how you hand a melody to a collaborator who hates your synth choice.

## The ninety percent you're allowed to ignore

Automation curves. Warp markers. Groove pools. Bus routing. Mid-side processing. Every one of those is genuinely useful, and every one of them exists to solve a problem you have not encountered yet.

The producers you admire didn't learn those features and then make music. They made music badly, hit a wall, went looking for a specific tool, learned that one tool, and went back to making music. That loop — make, hit wall, learn one thing — is the only method that has ever worked. Tutorial marathons are a way of feeling productive without exposing yourself to the risk of making something bad.

So: open a browser tab. Put drums in a lane. Draw eight notes in the piano roll. Turn the drums down until the notes are audible. Export it as a WAV. That took ten minutes and you used all four things, which means you have now, in the only sense that matters, learned a DAW.

If you want a shortcut on the harmony side while you're doing it, chord recipes drop a real progression onto a track as editable notes with study notes attached — and there's a whole library of samples, presets, and finished songs from other people at [the 100Lights community](https://100lights.com/community) that you can pull apart to see how they're built. Reverse-engineering someone's arrangement teaches faster than any course.

The grey grid is not an exam. It's graph paper.
