# 5 Chord Progressions Every Producer Should Know

Before you read a word of theory, do this. Put on the last three songs you loved. Listen to the chorus of each and count how many chords go by before the section repeats.

I'll wait.

It was four, wasn't it. Almost always four, occasionally three, rarely more. And here's the part that should bother you slightly: across those three songs, written by different people in different decades, at least two of them were probably the *same* four chords.

That's not laziness. It's the thing nobody tells you when they hand you a scale diagram: harmony in popular music is a very small vocabulary used very well. Most theory you can postpone for years. This part you can't, and it takes an afternoon.

@ear Play the chorus of a song you love. Hum the note it keeps returning to — the one that feels like the floor. That note is the key, and every progression below is measured from it.

Every progression here exists as a drag-and-drop recipe in the [100Lights](https://100lights.com) library, so you can hear each one inside a real project and pull the notes apart yourself.

## 1. The one you've already heard ten thousand times

Play this before you read what it is.

@progression(%7B%22chords%22%3A%5B%7B%22name%22%3A%22C%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B60%2C64%2C67%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A0%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22G%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B59%2C62%2C67%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A4%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22Am%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B57%2C60%2C64%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A8%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22F%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B57%2C60%2C65%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A12%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%5D%2C%22originalKey%22%3A0%7D) Play I–V–vi–IV and change the key

You recognised it in under two seconds. Everyone does. This is **I–V–vi–IV** — C, G, Am, F — and it is underneath *Let It Be*, *With or Without You*, and *Don't Stop Believin'*, along with a truly embarrassing number of records you'd defend in an argument.

@theory Home, then tension, then the bittersweet twin of home, then a lift back. What makes it glide rather than jump is that neighbouring chords share notes — C and G both contain G, Am and F both contain A and C. Your ear hears one voice moving rather than four chords colliding.

Use it when you want instant familiar warmth. Verse or chorus, any tempo, any genre.

## 2. The same chords, wearing black

Now play this one, and notice how much sadder it is.

@progression(%7B%22chords%22%3A%5B%7B%22name%22%3A%22Am%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B57%2C60%2C64%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A0%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22F%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B57%2C60%2C65%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A4%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22C%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B60%2C64%2C67%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A8%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22G%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B59%2C62%2C67%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A12%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%5D%2C%22originalKey%22%3A9%7D) Play vi–IV–I–V and change the key

Here's the trick: **it is the same four chords.** Am, F, C, G. Nothing was added, nothing was removed, nothing was made minor that wasn't already. All that changed is which chord you started on.

@theory Whichever chord you land on first becomes "home" for your ear. Start on the minor one and the entire loop reorganises itself around sadness — the major chords stop sounding like the destination and start sounding like brief relief on the way back down.

This is the cheapest songwriting tool in existence and almost nobody uses it deliberately. Before you throw out a progression that isn't working, rotate it. *Zombie* lives here. So does *Save Tonight*. So does *Numb*.

## 3. The one that can't stop

@progression(%7B%22chords%22%3A%5B%7B%22name%22%3A%22C%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B60%2C64%2C67%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A0%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22Am%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B57%2C60%2C64%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A4%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22F%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B57%2C60%2C65%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A8%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22G%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B59%2C62%2C67%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A12%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%5D%2C%22originalKey%22%3A0%7D) Play I–vi–IV–V and change the key

Let it loop four times before you read on. Notice that it never actually finishes — it keeps handing itself back to the beginning.

That's **I–vi–IV–V**, the doo-wop changes, every fifty-year-old slow dance and a surprising amount of modern synthwave.

@theory The loop can't close because it ends on V, and V's whole personality is wanting to go home. It never gets there inside the loop, so the wheel turns forever. That unfinishedness is the feature — it's why the progression feels like circling rather than arriving.

*Stand By Me*. *Every Breath You Take*. *Perfect*. If you want nostalgia in any instrumentation, this is the shortest route to it. Swap the IV for a ii and the bass walks a smooth circle instead — instant jazz-standard ending.

## 4. The one with the held breath

@progression(%7B%22chords%22%3A%5B%7B%22name%22%3A%22Dm7%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B50%2C53%2C57%2C60%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A0%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22G7%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B43%2C47%2C50%2C53%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A4%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22Cmaj7%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B48%2C52%2C55%2C59%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A8%2C%22dur%22%3A8%7D%5D%2C%22originalKey%22%3A0%7D) Play ii–V–I and change the key

Three chords, and the last one lands like someone finally exhaling. This is the **ii–V–I**, the cadence jazz is built out of.

@ear Play the middle chord (G7) on its own, on a loop, and sit with it for thirty seconds. It gets uncomfortable. Now play the last chord. That relief is the entire mechanism.

@theory The middle chord contains two notes a tritone apart — the most unstable interval available to us, the one medieval theorists genuinely legislated against. Your ear treats it as a question. The final chord answers it, and the answer is why this cadence has been used to end sections for four hundred years.

@math The tritone in G7 is B and F, three whole steps apart. B pulls up a semitone to C, F pulls down a semitone to E, and those two motions land you inside Cmaj7. Every ii–V–I in every key is that same pair of semitone moves, transposed.

Use it when you want a section to *end*. Neo-soul, lo-fi, and house borrow it constantly for exactly that reason.

## 5. The staircase

@progression(%7B%22chords%22%3A%5B%7B%22name%22%3A%22Am%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B57%2C60%2C64%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A0%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22G%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B55%2C59%2C62%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A4%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22F%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B53%2C57%2C60%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A8%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%2C%7B%22name%22%3A%22E%22%2C%22pitches%22%3A%5B52%2C56%2C59%5D%2C%22beat%22%3A12%2C%22dur%22%3A4%7D%5D%2C%22originalKey%22%3A9%7D) Play i–VII–VI–V and change the key

Listen to the bottom note only. A, G, F, E — it walks downstairs, one step per chord, and that stairway *is* the progression.

This is the **Andalusian cadence**, and it's doing something sneaky at the end.

@ear Play the last chord twice: once as written, then with the middle note lowered by one semitone. The Spanish flavour evaporates completely. One note carries the entire character of the progression.

@theory The final chord is major in a minor world. That single borrowed note is out of key on purpose, and it's the reason this reads as flamenco rather than as a sad pop loop. Take it away and you have a perfectly nice progression that sounds like nothing in particular.

*Hit the Road Jack*. The intro to *Sultans of Swing*. *Stray Cat Strut*. Drama and momentum without needing a chorus lift.

## What to do with this

You now know four chords and roughly five ways to arrange them, which is genuinely more harmonic vocabulary than most working songwriters use in a career. The gap between knowing and using is smaller than you'd think, and it closes like this:

1. **Drag one in and loop it.** The Recipes tab in the library has all five, sorted by genre, each with notes on the mechanism.
2. **Mumble over it.** Melodies come from singing, not from staring at a grid. Record a hummed idea before you try to draw one.
3. **Rotate before you replace.** You heard what starting on a different chord did. Do that before reaching for new chords.
4. **Play only the bass notes.** If the progression still works with one note per chord, it's a good progression. If it doesn't, no amount of voicing will save it.

When you've made something out of one of these, put it in the [community](https://100lights.com/community) — half the value of a progression is hearing what somebody else did with the one you thought you understood.
