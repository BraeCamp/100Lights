# 5 Chord Progressions Every Producer Should Know

Most music theory you can postpone. Chord progressions you can't — they're the emotional skeleton of every track you've ever loved, and the same five keep showing up across seventy years of records. Learn these and you can sketch the harmony for almost anything. Every progression below exists as a drag-and-drop **recipe** in the [100Lights](https://100lights.com) library, so you can hear each one inside a real project and edit the notes yourself.

## 1. The pop progression — I–V–vi–IV

**C → G → Am → F.** The four chords under a thousand hits. I (C) feels like home, V (G) adds tension, vi (Am) turns it bittersweet, IV (F) lifts back toward home. The voicings share notes between neighbors — C and G share a G; Am and F share both A and C — and that overlap is why the changes feel smooth instead of jumpy.

**Use it when:** you want instant, familiar warmth. Verse or chorus, any tempo, any genre.

**Twist it:** reorder to vi–IV–I–V (start on the minor chord) — same four chords, completely different mood. That variant has its own name…

## 2. The axis progression — vi–IV–I–V

**Am → F → C → G.** The pop progression rotated to start on its saddest chord, which makes the whole loop moodier without changing a single note. Half of modern melancholy pop lives here.

**Use it when:** you want emotional but still radio-shaped. It loops beautifully under four-bar melodies.

**Twist it:** compare it side by side with the pop progression and the "big-chorus" rotation (IV–I–V–vi) — three moods from the identical four chords. Rotation is the cheapest songwriting tool there is.

## 3. Doo-wop changes — I–vi–IV–V

**C → Am → F → G.** Every 50s slow dance. The signature move is the vi chord *right after* home — you establish comfort, then immediately show its melancholy twin. The V at the end never resolves inside the loop, which is exactly what makes it circular: the pull back to C restarts the wheel.

**Use it when:** you want nostalgia — it reads as "classic" in any instrumentation, from piano ballad to synthwave.

**Twist it:** swap the IV for a ii (making I–vi–ii–V) and the bass suddenly walks a smooth circle of fifths — instant jazz-standard ending.

## 4. The jazz ii–V–I — Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7

The cadence jazz is built on. Sevenths everywhere — the extra note in each chord is what makes it "jazz"; delete the 7ths and it turns plain. The engine underneath: G7 contains both B and F, a tritone, the most unstable interval we have, so landing on Cmaj7 feels like a held breath released.

**Use it when:** you want a section to *end* — the ii–V–I is punctuation. Neo-soul, lo-fi, and house records borrow it constantly for exactly that.

**Twist it:** move the whole thing up a whole step (Em7 → A7 → Dmaj7). Same engine, new key — chain the two and you're modulating like a jazz tune.

## 5. The Andalusian cadence — i–VII–VI–V

**Am → G → F → E.** Flamenco's descending walk. The bass falls one step per chord — A, G, F, E — and that stairway down *is* the progression. The last chord is E **major** in an A-minor world; the borrowed G# is the exotic color that makes it unmistakable.

**Use it when:** you want drama and momentum without a chorus lift — it powers everything from flamenco to surf rock to hip-hop loops.

**Twist it:** make the final E minor instead and listen to the Spanish flavor evaporate. One note carries the whole character.

## How to actually use these

1. **Drag one in.** In the studio's library, open the Recipes tab — the progressions above live in genre folders, each with a playable preview and notes on why it works.
2. **Loop it and mumble.** Melodies come from singing over chords, not from staring at a piano roll. Record any topline idea, even a hummed one.
3. **Rotate before you replace.** If a progression isn't working, try starting it on a different chord before reaching for new chords — you heard what rotation did to the pop progression.
4. **Steal the bass.** Play only the lowest note of each chord on a bass track. If the progression still works, it's a good progression.

All five are free to use in the [browser studio](https://100lights.com) — drag a recipe onto a track and start pulling notes around. Theory sticks a lot faster when it's under your own drums.
