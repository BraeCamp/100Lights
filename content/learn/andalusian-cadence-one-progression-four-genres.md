# One Progression, Four Genres: The Andalusian Cadence

Before you read anything else, play these four chords. One per bar, hold each one all the way through, no rhythm, no drums.

A minor. G major. F major. E major.

Play it and listen for where it stops sounding like ordinary sad music and starts sounding like *somewhere*.

If you don't have an instrument in front of you, open a project in [100Lights](https://100lights.com), drag the *Andalusian cadence* recipe from the library onto a track, and press play. It lands as regular notes in the piano roll, so everything below is editable.

@ear Play all four bars twice through. Don't analyze it. Just answer one question: at which chord does the loop stop sounding like ordinary sad music and start sounding like *somewhere*?

Most people say the fourth one. Hold that thought. We're not explaining it yet.

## Now play it as flamenco

Same four chords. Change nothing about which notes are in them. Change this instead:

Give each chord a strummed rhythm instead of a held block — short, on the beat, then two quick ones off the beat. Put the chords high, near the middle of the keyboard, and stack the top note as a doubled octave. Put a nylon-guitar or plucked preset on the track. Let each chord ring only about half a bar, then leave silence.

@ear With the rhythm in, listen specifically to the *bottom* note of each chord as it moves. A, then G, then F, then E. Say those out loud as they pass. Does that shape remind you of a staircase or a circle?

Staircase. Every time. It only goes down, one step at a time, and it never turns around.

## Now play it as surf rock

Delete the strum. Give every chord exactly eight even eighth notes, machine-steady. Drop the whole thing an octave. Swap the preset to something bright and thin with a fast attack. Add reverb on the track — a lot of it, more than feels tasteful, because tasteful is not what 1962 was doing.

You have just written the skeleton of a thousand surf and spy-theme cues — the Andalusian cadence played by a man in a hurry.

@ear Loop your surf version four times without stopping. Somewhere around the third pass you'll notice you don't want it to end on the fourth chord — you want it to go around again. Note that feeling. It's going to be the whole point.

## Now play it as metal

Take only the lowest note of each chord. Just one note. A, G, F, E, down in the bass register. Give each one a chugging sixteenth-note rhythm with a distorted or heavily saturated sound. Add the fifth above each root if you want the weight; leave out the third entirely.

The chords are gone. The progression isn't. That's not a small thing — you deleted two thirds of every chord and the identity survived, which tells you where the identity was actually living.

## Now play it as pop

Slow it down. Give each chord a full bar again, held. Put a soft pad underneath, an arpeggio on top that just cycles through each chord's notes as eighth notes, and a kick on every beat. Keep the bass moving A, G, F, E.

@ear Play the pop version and the flamenco version back to back, one after the other, in the same project. Two tracks, same four chords, muted alternately. Ask a friend if it's the same progression. They will hesitate.

Four genres. One harmonic sentence. Nothing changed but voicing, rhythm, register, and instrument.

## Here's what you've been hearing

@theory Two separate things are happening, and they're doing different jobs.

The first is the descent. The lowest note falls by one step, then another step, then another, and a falling bass line reads to the ear as gravity — as something settling, giving way, running out. It doesn't matter what's stacked above it. A listener who could not name a single chord will still feel a floor sliding downward. That's why the metal version works with the chords stripped away: the staircase was carrying the whole thing.

The second is the last chord, and this is the part that makes it *Spanish* rather than merely sad. Three of the four chords belong to the same gloomy family. The fourth one arrives with a note that has no business being there — a note lifted from outside the neighborhood, brighter than everything around it, and sitting a half step below home. It glares. It leans. It wants, badly, to fall back to the first chord, and because the progression loops, it always gets what it wants and then immediately wants it again.

That's the engine. A soft slide down, then a hard bright yank back to the top.

@ear Now the experiment that proves it. Take your original clip and change the last chord from major to minor — lower its middle note by one key. Play the loop. The staircase is still there. The Spain is gone. It sounds like a folk song from somewhere cold.

Put it back. Feel it return.

@math For the arithmetic-inclined: in the Andalusian cadence as loaded from the recipe, the four bass roots are MIDI 57, 55, 53, 52 — intervals of −2, −2, −1 semitones. The final E major triad is 52, 56, 59; the E minor version is 52, 55, 59. The single note that carries the entire flavor is the difference between 55 and 56. One semitone, one key on the piano roll, and a whole geography.

## Why the loop refuses to end

Go back to that surf-rock moment where you didn't want it to stop. Here's the mechanism.

@theory A normal progression ends by arriving somewhere and staying. This one ends by arriving somewhere that *points* — the last chord is a question, not a period. So the loop never closes. Every time around, the final chord hands you back to the first chord as if it were doing you a favor, and the descent starts over. This is why flamenco can sit on these four chords for eleven minutes, and why a producer can loop them for four bars under a chorus without anyone getting bored. The progression is a wheel with no flat side.

It also means the cadence is terrible at endings. If you want to actually stop, you have to stop on the first chord and let the fourth one resolve into it one final time — which is exactly what every song built on it does.

## What to do with this

Build all four versions in one project. Four tracks, identical notes, different presets and rhythms, and use the mixer to solo them one at a time. You'll learn more about arrangement in twenty minutes of that than in a month of reading, because you'll have removed harmony as a variable and been forced to hear everything else.

Then break it on purpose:

- Reverse it — E, F, G, Am — and hear a climb instead of a fall. Same chords, opposite emotion.
- Hold the third chord for two bars instead of one and let the arrival at the fourth land harder.
- Keep the descending bass but replace the chords above it with something else entirely. The staircase will still read.

All of it runs in the browser for free — no plugins, no install — and when you land on a version worth keeping, export the stems or publish the recipe to the [community](https://100lights.com/community) so someone else can take your four chords and make a fifth genre out of them.

Four chords. That's all it ever was.
