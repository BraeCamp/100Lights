# How Music Is Counted: Bars and Beats

You were told that counting music is a thing trained musicians do and you don't. That the "one-two-three-four" is for conservatory kids and drum teachers, and that you, the self-taught bedroom producer, should just feel it. This is the single worst piece of advice in the whole beginner canon, and it's why your arrangements wander.

Here's the reversal. Counting is not advanced theory. It's the most basic literacy there is, more basic than knowing a single chord, and you already do it without noticing — your foot does it in the car. The grid at the top of every DAW, that ruler with the numbers, is not decoration for people who read sheet music. It is the map of the song. You've been staring at a map and refusing to read the legend.

## The beat is the thing your foot already knows

Put on almost any song with drums and tap your foot. You'll land on a steady, even pulse — not on every drum hit, but on the underlying tick the whole thing hangs off. That's the beat. It's the heartbeat of the track, and your body finds it in about two seconds without being asked.

That steadiness is the whole point. The beat doesn't speed up or slow down inside a song (mostly), which is exactly why it works as a ruler. Producers measure tempo in beats per minute — BPM. A slow ballad might sit at 70 BPM. House music lives around 120 to 128. Drum and bass runs near 174. When someone says a track is "128," they mean the beat ticks 128 times a minute, and if you tapped your foot to it, that's the number your foot is hitting.

You don't count beats forever, though. That would be exhausting and useless. You count them in small handfuls.

## The bar is a handful of beats

Keep tapping, but now count your taps out loud: one, two, three, four, one, two, three, four. Notice that "one" keeps wanting to land in the same place — usually where the music feels like it resets, where the bassline restarts or the drum pattern comes back around.

That group of four beats is a bar. Also called a measure. It's just a box that holds four beats, and songs are built out of these boxes stacked end to end. The reason you naturally counted to four and not to five or seven is that the overwhelming majority of popular music — pop, rock, hip-hop, house, country, the lot — puts four beats in every bar. It's so standard that musicians call it common time.

Written down, common time is "4/4." The top number tells you how many beats are in a bar: four. The bottom number tells you which kind of note gets called a beat, and you can ignore it for about a year without any harm coming to you. Four beats per bar. That's the sentence that matters.

@theory The reason "one" feels different from the other three beats has a name: it's the downbeat, the strong pulse the bar leans on. Beats two and four usually carry the snare — the backbeat — which is why clapping along to a song feels natural on two and four but wrong on one and three. Your body isn't confused. It's tracking the same structure the producer built, and the pull you feel toward the downbeat is the bar announcing where it starts.

## Why everything comes in eights

Now the part nobody explains, the part that turns counting from a party trick into an actual production skill.

Bars group up. And they group up in a spookily consistent way: fours and eights. Count the bars from the start of a verse to the start of the chorus in almost any pop song and you'll land on eight, or sixteen. Intros are often four or eight bars. The build before a drop in dance music is famously eight or sixteen. This isn't a rule someone enforces — it's a habit so deep that breaking it feels like a statement.

Which is the trick. Once you know sections come in eights, you can hear a song's skeleton before you understand a note of its harmony. You stop asking "what should happen next" and start asking "I'm four bars in, I've got four to go before this needs to change." That's arrangement. That's the entire game, and it's counting, which you were told not to bother with.

@ear Play a song you know cold. Count the bars — one-two-three-four is one bar, then start again for bar two — from the first downbeat of the verse until the chorus hits. Don't overthink where the verse "starts"; go with where the groove settles. You'll almost certainly count eight bars, or sixteen. Now go count the loop you've been stuck on. If it changes every two bars, that's why it feels frantic. If it never changes across sixteen, that's why it feels dead.

@math At 120 BPM, one beat lasts half a second (60 divided by 120). A 4/4 bar is four of those, so two seconds. Eight bars is sixteen seconds — which is why an eight-bar loop at that tempo gives you a tidy sixteen-second phrase, and why so much dance music, built around 120–128 BPM, breathes in these neat chunks. Change the tempo and the seconds change, but the counting never does. Eight bars is always eight bars.

## Not everything is four

Some music refuses the four, and knowing that four is a choice — not a law of physics — is worth more than it looks.

A waltz counts to three: one-two-three, one-two-three. That's 3/4, and "The Blue Danube" is the textbook case; the lilt you associate with ballroom dancing is just a bar with three beats instead of four. Dave Brubeck's "Take Five" is in five — count it and you'll get one-two-three-four-five, over and over, which is what makes it sound restless and slightly wrong in a good way. The main groove of Pink Floyd's "Money" runs in seven. Once you can count, these oddities stop being mysterious. They're just bars with a different number of beats in the box.

You'll write in 4/4 for a long time, and that's correct. But now you know why the strange songs feel strange. They're counted differently.

You don't need to trust your foot alone. 100Lights runs entirely in the browser — no download — and it shows you the count directly.

Look at the top of the arrangement: that numbered ruler is the bar and beat ruler. Bar 1, bar 2, bar 3, each divided into its four beats. As the song plays, the playhead crosses it, and you can watch "8 bars" happen as a physical distance on screen instead of an idea in your head. When you drop a loop and it lands exactly from bar 1 to bar 5, you've made a four-bar clip, and you can see it.

Turn on the metronome and it clicks the beat for you — a sharper click on the downbeat, so "one" is unmistakable. Hit record and the count-in ticks you a bar before recording starts, so you come in on the downbeat instead of chasing it. Play, tap, count out loud along with the click until the four stops being something you count and becomes something you feel. That's the whole goal: the counting goes into your body and then disappears.
