# Your Beat Sounds Stiff Because You Quantized It

Here's the advice everyone gives a beginner whose drums feel wrong: quantize them. Snap every hit to the grid, tighten it up, get it perfect. So you did. Now every note lands exactly on the line, dead center, machine-locked — and your beat sounds like a metronome having a seizure. It's not sloppy anymore. It's worse. It's stiff.

Perfect timing is not good timing. It never was. The records you're trying to sound like were made by people, or by people faking people, and not one of them lands every hit on the grid. Quantize didn't fix your beat. It removed the only thing that made it feel like music, and now you're looking for the setting that puts it back.

## The crime the grid committed

A grid divides the bar into equal slices. Straight sixteenths means four hits per beat, evenly spaced, each one the same distance from the last. That's the default in every DAW, including this one, and it's fine for a demo of what a drum machine can do.

The problem is that no groove worth copying is evenly spaced. When a drummer plays a hi-hat pattern, the off-beats — the notes *between* the main beats — don't land halfway. They land a hair late. That lateness is not a mistake the drummer is trying to correct. It *is* the feel. It's the difference between a beat that marches and a beat that walks.

Quantize takes that hair of lateness and deletes it. You asked it to. Now you want it back, and the honest name for wanting it back is swing.

## What swing actually does

Swing delays the off-beats. That's the whole mechanism, and it's smaller than you think.

Take a pair of subdivisions — call them the "one" and the "and." Straight timing puts the "and" exactly in the middle, halfway between one beat and the next. Swing pushes that "and" later, closer to the beat that follows it. The down-beats don't move. Only the in-between notes slide back. Long-short, long-short: the first note of each pair gets more room, the second gets squeezed toward the end.

Do that across a whole pattern and the beat starts to lope. The hats stop sounding like a ruler and start sounding like a limp with a purpose.

@theory Why does a late note feel better than an on-time one? Because tension in rhythm comes from expectation, and a note that arrives exactly when predicted releases no tension at all — it just confirms the grid. Nudge that note late and the ear leans forward waiting for it, then relaxes when it lands. That tiny lean-and-release, happening on every off-beat, several times a bar, is what people are describing when they say a beat has feel. Even spacing gives the ear nothing to lean on. That's why it feels dead even when every sound in it is great.

In 100Lights there's a SWING control right in the transport bar at the top. Drag it up and it delays the off-beat subdivisions of the whole project for you — no per-note editing, no math. That's the fast version, and it's genuinely one slider.

@math If you want to know what the slider is doing: swing is usually described as a percentage, where fifty percent means straight — the off-beat sits exactly halfway. Push higher and the off-beat moves later. At ninety beats per minute, one beat lasts about 667 ms, so a pair of sixteenth notes spans roughly 333 ms. At fifty percent the second sixteenth lands at about 167 ms into that pair. At sixty percent it lands near 200 ms — about thirty-three milliseconds late. At around sixty-seven percent it reaches the triplet position near 222 ms, roughly fifty-five milliseconds late, which is the hard, obvious shuffle. Thirty to fifty milliseconds is a big deal. That's the whole range most grooves live in.

## The number, and why cranking it is a mistake

More swing is not more groove. This is where people go wrong the moment they find the slider.

Fifty percent is straight. Somewhere around fifty-four to sixty-two percent is where most modern beats actually sit — enough to hear, not enough to announce itself. Around sixty-six or sixty-seven percent you hit a full triplet shuffle, the thing your ear reads as "swung" without being told. Past that it gets seasick.

Crank it to maximum and you don't get a groovier beat. You get a novelty. The skill isn't turning the knob up; it's finding the two or three percent where the beat locks in and stops sounding like either a robot or a drunk. That spot is different for every tempo and every pattern, which is why there's no correct number — there's only the number that's right for this beat, found by ear.

## Where swing lives

Different genres put their swing in different places, and calling it all "swing" hides that.

Jazz swings the eighth notes, hard, toward a triplet feel — the classic long-short "swung eighths" that a ride cymbal spells out. Interesting wrinkle: as jazz tempos get faster, players straighten those eighths out almost to even, because at speed a hard shuffle turns to mush. So swing isn't a fixed ratio even inside one tradition.

Hip-hop mostly swings the sixteenths, and more loosely. This is Dilla territory — beats that feel like they're leaning back, dragging just behind where you expect. House and its relatives put the swing largely in the hi-hats: a subtle sixteenth-note push under an otherwise straight kick, which is a lot of what separates a house groove from a stiff four-on-the-floor. UK garage takes the same idea and shoves it much further, swinging the sixteenths so hard the whole rhythm skips.

@ear Program a plain sixteenth-note hi-hat, straight, and loop it. Now raise the SWING slider slowly while it plays. Listen for the moment the pattern stops sounding like a typewriter and starts to bounce — and then the later moment it tips into a limp that's too much. The good zone is the gap between those two. Mark where it is for this tempo. It'll move when you change the tempo.

## Swing is one number. Groove isn't.

Here's the limit of the slider, and nobody selling you a swing preset will mention it. Global swing applies the *same* delay to *every* off-beat. Real drummers don't do that. Their lateness varies — this hat a little behind, that one almost on time, the snare sitting in its own pocket independent of the hats.

That variation has a name too: humanizing, or micro-timing. Dilla's drums are the famous case. People call his timing "drunk" because his hits weren't uniformly swung — they were individually placed, some early, some late, the kick and snare pulling against each other in a way no single swing value can reproduce. It sounds human because it was, hit by hit.

You can do a version of this by hand right here. Turn snap off in the piano roll or the arrangement and drag individual notes a few pixels off the grid — the snare a touch late, one hat early, leave the kick alone. It's slower than the slider and it's where the actual feel is. Global swing gets you eighty percent of the way in one drag. The last twenty percent is you, moving notes one at a time, deciding what leans and what doesn't.

I resisted this for years. I thought timing was something you either had or fixed with quantize, and both of those are the same lie from opposite ends. Timing is a set of decisions about which notes are allowed to be late. You make them, or the grid makes them for you, and the grid always decides "none," which is exactly why your beat sounds the way it does.

Try both — one drag of the slider, then five minutes of nudging by hand — export them, and play them back to back. If you can't hear it yet, post both to the [community](/community) and ask which one grooves. People will pick the same one, and it won't be the quantized one.
