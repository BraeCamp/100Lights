# You Have Nine Unfinished Projects

Here is what happened. You opened a project on a Tuesday. You made a drum pattern, and it was good — genuinely, it was good, that hat pattern had something. You added a bass. You added chords. You had eight bars.

Then you listened to it. Then you listened to it again. Then you listened to it thirty-eight more times.

Somewhere around listen twenty you stopped hearing it as music and started hearing it as a problem. The kick sounded thin. You spent forty minutes on the kick. The kick now sounds different, not better. You solved nothing, and you know you solved nothing, and the loop has stopped meaning anything to you at all.

So you opened a new project.

You have nine of those. Some of them are two years old. You've named exactly one of them — the rest are called "Untitled," "Untitled 2," and, at some point in a burst of optimism, "IDEA GOOD."

Let's fix this one.

## The loop is not the problem

You think you're stuck because the loop isn't finished. It's finished. Eight bars of drums, bass, and chords is a complete musical idea. What you're actually stuck on is that you've never once done the next part, so you don't know what the next part is, so your brain — which is very good at avoiding unfamiliar work — hands you a fresh project instead. It feels like inspiration. It is avoidance wearing inspiration's coat.

The thing nobody tells you: arranging is boring. Not hard. Boring. It's copy, paste, delete, mute, listen, undo. There's no dopamine in it. The first eight bars are the fun part and you've been eating dessert for two years.

@theory A loop stops rewarding you not because it got worse but because your ear has finished mapping it. Once you can predict every event before it arrives, the music has no more information to give you, and your brain reads that as "bad." It isn't bad. It's known. A first-time listener would still be in the interesting part.

## Rule one: pick a deadline that embarrasses you

Not a goal. A deadline. Goals are decorative.

Give yourself one session — two hours, one sitting — to take an existing loop from eight bars to a two-minute arrangement that has a beginning, a middle, and an end. Two hours is deliberately not enough time to do it well. That's the point. You cannot fix the kick in two hours, so you won't try.

If you need help getting there, the crudest arrangement that works is this: intro with one element removed, your loop, your loop with something added, a section where the drums drop out entirely, your loop back with everything, and an ending that just stops. That's it. That's an arrangement. Copy your section, paste it five times, then go through and take things away.

Taking away is the whole job. In [100Lights](https://100lights.com) you can select across every track at once and tile a section, so building the skeleton takes about ninety seconds. The remaining hour and fifty-eight minutes is muting things and listening.

## Rule two: bounce it while it's ugly

Export a WAV the moment you have two minutes of anything. Not when it's good. When it exists.

This does something specific that nothing else does. A rendered file leaves the studio and enters the world as a piece of music, and you will hear it differently the instant it does — in the car, on your phone, on the terrible laptop speakers. You will hear three real problems and you will stop hearing the forty fake problems you invented while staring at the timeline.

Also: you now have a finished song. Bad, but finished. That's one. You have never had one.

Bounce again at every session. Keep them. Number them. Version four will sound better than version one in ways you'd never have noticed otherwise, and on the day you're convinced you're not improving, version one is sitting right there being worse.

## Rule three: someone else has to hear it

The private project folder is where songs go to die. Nine of them are in there right now.

You don't need an audience — you need one person with ears and a deadline attached to them. Send a friend a link and a date. Post the thing to the [100Lights community](https://100lights.com/community) and let it be public and imperfect. It doesn't matter which. What matters is that the moment someone else is expecting it, the internal argument about whether the kick is thin enough ends, because it has to.

Nobody has ever noticed your kick. I want to be gentle about this. In the entire history of a listener hearing a track for the first time, the kick has never once been the thing. They notice whether it goes somewhere.

## Rule four: declare it done out loud

There is no moment when a track becomes finished. It never happens. Producers with credits you'd recognize don't experience it either — they experience a delivery date, which arrives whether or not the snare is right.

So you have to do it artificially. Set a rule now, before you're inside the work: when the arrangement has a beginning, a middle, and an end, and the levels are balanced enough that nothing makes you flinch, it's done. Not good. Done. Export the WAV, export the stems as a zip so future-you can revisit it, put the file somewhere permanent, and close the project.

Then — and this is the part that actually matters — do not open it again for thirty days.

@ear Play your last finished bounce next to a commercial track you love, at the same volume, and don't listen for quality. Listen for *events*: count how many times something new arrives in the first sixty seconds of each. That gap, not the mix, is what you're hearing when you think yours sounds "unfinished."

## What changes

You will finish a bad song. It will be genuinely bad in a way you can hear clearly, which is a different experience from having nine loops you privately suspect were great.

Then you'll finish another one, and it'll be bad in a *different* way, and that's the actual mechanism — you can only fix problems that survive long enough to be identified. A loop hides its problems. An arrangement can't. Everything wrong with your music is downstream of never having reached the part where things go wrong.

Nine projects, two years, no songs. Open the oldest one. Not the best one — the oldest one, the one you already stopped caring about, because you can't ruin something you've abandoned. Give it two hours and a hard stop. Make it bad on purpose.

You can do the whole thing — arrange, mix, export, share — free in the browser, which means the only cost is that you have to stop starting.
