# You Don't Need Better Gear

You have eleven tabs open. Four of them are audio interfaces. Two are the same audio interface on different retailers because you're checking whether one is nineteen dollars cheaper. One is a forum thread from 2019 where a stranger says the preamps are "a bit veiled." You don't know what veiled means. You have decided it's bad.

There's a review video paused at 4:12 on the second monitor. It's forty-one minutes long and you've watched thirty of them. The reviewer is holding two microphones and saying they're both excellent.

You have not opened a project today.

Somewhere in a folder is a track you made eight months ago, and if you're honest with yourself — do be honest, nobody's watching — nothing about it would be improved by a nineteen-dollar-cheaper preamp. It has no chorus. That's the problem with it. It has no chorus and the vocal sits four decibels too low, and neither of those things lives in a shopping cart.

Close the tabs. Let's talk about what's actually happening.

## The gear search is a real feeling about a fake problem

Here's why it's so seductive: it feels like work. It has research, comparison, decisions, expertise. You get better at it over time — you genuinely do know more about converters than you did last year. It's the shape of progress with none of the exposure.

Making music doesn't feel like that. Making music feels like sitting in front of an eight-bar loop knowing something's wrong and having no idea what. That's an unpleasant, ego-free, blank state and no amount of it ever feels like expertise.

So the brain, being an efficient animal, goes shopping.

I'm not above this. I bought a compressor in 2016 specifically because a producer I admired had one in a photo. It's a fine compressor. I used it on maybe four songs. The songs weren't better. I got better about three years later, and it had nothing to do with the compressor — it had to do with finally admitting my arrangements were flat.

## What's actually limiting you, in order

Not opinions. Order of magnitude.

**One: the arrangement.** Ninety percent of "my track sounds amateur" is that nothing happens. Same eight bars, same density, start to finish. A commercial track changes something every eight bars, often something tiny — a hat pattern shifts, a synth drops out for two beats, a reverb tail opens up. Your loop is fine. Your loop is also all you have.

**Two: the balance.** Not "the mix" — the balance. Faders only. Most amateur mixes have three or four elements fighting at the same level, and the fix is dragging things down until there is an obvious loudest thing, an obvious second thing, and everything else supporting. That's not gear. That's a decision you've been avoiding because it means admitting something you spent an hour on should be quiet.

**Three: finishing.** You can't develop taste on unfinished work. Ten finished mediocre songs will teach you more than a hundred loops and a better interface.

**Four, distantly:** sound sources. And even here, the free 1000+ sound library in [100Lights](https://100lights.com) will outrun your ability to use it well for a long while yet. Nobody has ever identified a sample library from a finished record.

**Five, so far down it's practically invisible:** your microphone.

@theory The reason an upgraded mic changes so little is that a recording's character is dominated by the room it happened in and the distance you sat from the capsule. Reflections arriving a few milliseconds after the direct sound get heard as "cheap" or "boxy," and that arrives identically through a budget capsule and an expensive one. You are not hearing the microphone. You are hearing the wall behind you.

## Test it before you believe me

@ear Take your last track. Pull every fader down to nothing, then bring up only the loudest element until it's comfortable. Add the next element and stop the moment you can hear it clearly — not when it feels good, when it's merely audible. Continue through every track. Do not touch an EQ. Play the result against the original.

Most people find the new one sounds bigger. Not because anything got louder — because things stopped competing. If that just happened to you, you now have direct evidence that your problem was never the signal chain.

## What genuinely is worth money, eventually

Eventually. After you've finished a handful of songs and you can hear specific things you can't fix. In this order.

**Headphones, first and by a wide margin.** Not expensive ones — accurate ones, somewhere in the hundred-to-two-hundred range, open-backed if your room is quiet. This is the only purchase that changes every single decision you make forever. Everything else improves one part of the chain. Headphones improve your judgment. Get one good pair, then use them for four years until you know exactly how they lie, because knowing how your monitoring lies is worth more than accuracy.

**Room treatment, second.** Deeply unsexy, no reviews to watch, cannot be photographed. A few thick panels at the first reflection points and something soft behind your head will do more for your recordings and your mixes than any microphone under a thousand dollars. If you're recording vocals, a heavy blanket on a stand behind the singer costs almost nothing and removes most of what makes home recordings sound like home recordings.

That's the list. That's genuinely the whole list for the next two years. An interface if you don't own one and need to record real instruments — but "own one" is the bar, not "own a better one."

## The uncomfortable part

Everything above is doable, start to finish, in a browser tab that costs nothing: arrange it, balance it, record over it, export the WAV, share it. Which removes the last excuse, and that's exactly why the shopping is so appealing — as long as the interface is the problem, you're a producer waiting on a delivery rather than a producer avoiding an arrangement.

Go look at what people are actually posting in the [100Lights community](https://100lights.com/community). Some of it is very good. None of it is good because of a preamp.

Now close the eleven tabs, open the eight-month-old project, and give it a chorus.
