# Ten Licks Worth Stealing

Here's something working writers know and rarely admit in interviews: nobody composes a section from nothing. They deploy a move. A two-bar gesture with a known emotional effect, borrowed from a tradition, dropped into place because it does a specific job and does it reliably.

Session players call these licks. Arrangers call them devices. Producers mostly don't call them anything, because naming them would make it obvious how few there are.

What follows is ten of them. Each one is short — most fit in two bars — and each one has carried entire records on its own. You have heard all ten. You have probably never separated them out from the songs they were embedded in, which is precisely why they keep working on you.

Every one of these can be played into a piano roll in the free browser studio at [100Lights](https://100lights.com) in under a minute. That's the whole assignment: play them badly, then play them wrong on purpose.

## 1. The gospel amen turn

Move to the fourth chord and fall back home. That's it — in C, an F to a C, with the top voice stepping down. It's the "a-men" at the end of every hymn you were made to sing, and it's why "Let It Be" can end without a proper cadence and still feel finished.

Use it when a section needs to conclude without drama. It settles instead of arriving.

@theory This turn sounds like relief rather than resolution because the chord it comes from doesn't contain the restless note that normally pulls you home. Nothing is straining. It's the difference between a door closing and a door being pushed shut, and it's why it reads as devotional rather than triumphant.

## 2. The phrygian low riff

Root, then the note immediately above it, then back. Played low, played slowly, played with total confidence. This is the flamenco cadence, the Andalusian sound, and it is the entire basis of "Misirlou," of Metallica's "Wherever I May Roam," of about eighty percent of soundtrack cues that need to signal danger.

Keep it on one string, so to speak. The move loses everything if you harmonize it.

## 3. The pedal-point lift

Hold one bass note. Change the chords above it. Do not move the bass. Composers have been doing this since Bach's organ music and it never stops working — U2 built the entire chorus of "With or Without You" over a droning D while the harmony wandered off and came back.

The reason to steal this one: it lets you introduce chords that would sound unrelated over a moving bass. The drone glues them together.

@ear Play a low A and hold it for eight bars. Over the top, play Am, then F, then G, then E — deliberately more motion than the section wants. Then play the same four chords with the bass following the roots. The second version is fine. The first version sounds like a film.

## 4. The bossa comp

Jobim's rhythm: the chord doesn't land on the beat, it lands just before it. In "The Girl from Ipanema" almost every chord is anticipated by an eighth note, which is why the music floats instead of marching.

Take any four-chord loop you already have. Drag every chord one eighth earlier. Leave the bass where it is. The whole thing loosens.

## 5. The trap minor cascade

A minor arpeggio falling fast in triplets, usually on something bright and thin — a flute, a bell, a plucked string patch. Metro Boomin and Southside built a decade of records on it. The flute figure in Future's "Mask Off" is the canonical version.

The trick nobody mentions is the register. Play it too low and it's a bass run; the cascade only works when it's floating well above everything else and the bottom of the track is nearly empty.

## 6. The quartal rise

Stack notes a fourth apart instead of a third. Three of them, climbing. McCoy Tyner did this constantly behind Coltrane, and Bill Evans' voicing on "So What" — the sound of modal jazz in one chord — is fourths with a third on top.

Fourths are the cheapest way to make a chord sound like it belongs to no particular key. Use them when a progression is getting too obedient.

@math A major third spans four semitones; a perfect fourth spans five. Stack three fourths from D and you land on D, G, C, F — every note of D minor eleven, with no third anywhere to tell you whether you're happy or sad. That missing third is the whole effect.

## 7. The blues turnaround

The last two bars of a twelve-bar blues, where a descending line walks you back to the top. Robert Johnson's version is the ancestor; every Chicago record from the fifties uses some mutation of it.

Steal it for the last two bars of *any* loop, blues or not. A section that turns around asks to repeat. A section that just stops asks to be over.

## 8. The minor waltz

Three beats, minor key, emphasis firmly on beat one. "My Favorite Things" once Coltrane got hold of it. Elliott Smith's "Waltz #2." Almost every music box in every horror trailer ever cut.

Almost nothing in modern production is in three. That scarcity is the opportunity — an eight-bar waltz section inside a 4/4 track is one of the most disorienting things you can do to a listener without changing a single sound.

## 9. The pentatonic cascade

Five notes, no half-steps, running downward across two or three octaves. Because a pentatonic scale contains no adjacent semitones, you genuinely cannot play a wrong note in it, which is why harpists and koto players and every producer who's ever needed an "ancient" texture reach for the same figure.

Play it fast enough and it stops being a melody and becomes a sound effect. That's usually the point.

@theory The reason a cascade like this can be sprinkled over almost any chord in the key without clashing is that the notes most likely to argue with the harmony have already been removed from the scale. You are working with a set that was pre-filtered for agreement. It's less a scale than a safety net.

## 10. The Moonlight broken minor

Beethoven's first movement: a minor triad broken into steady triplets, one note at a time, endlessly, under a melody that barely moves. C-sharp minor, 1801, and it has never gone out of use — Lennon said outright that "Because" came from playing the Moonlight Sonata's chords backwards.

Program a minor triad as continuous triplet arpeggios. Put a melody of long held notes on top. Do nothing else for eight bars. It will sound composed, and it took you ninety seconds.

## Where to put them

None of these are complete ideas. They're gestures, and a gesture needs somewhere to happen — which usually means an existing chord loop underneath it. The chord recipe library ships progressions you can drag straight onto a track as editable notes, each with a study note explaining why it works, and every lick above will sit on top of at least three of them.

Then, honestly: change them. Play the amen turn but land on the wrong bass note. Run the pentatonic cascade upward. The licks that become *yours* are the ones you broke in a way you liked and then couldn't undo. When one of them turns into something worth keeping, save it as a recipe and publish it to the [community](https://100lights.com/community) — the whole tradition of this stuff is theft in both directions.
