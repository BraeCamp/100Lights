# Session View vs Arrangement View (and When to Switch)

You didn't run out of ideas at bar 32. You committed to a timeline at bar 8, and every decision after that was made in service of a shape you picked before you knew what the song was.

This is the most expensive habit in modern production and almost nobody names it, because the default screen in almost every studio is a horizontal timeline running left to right. You open the program and it hands you a road. Roads suggest driving. So you drive — kick, then hat, then bass, then copy-paste to bar 17, then a fill, then the eight-bar breakdown you've built forty times before — and by the time you've got two minutes you have a structure and no song.

## The timeline is a commitment device, and you keep using it as a sketchpad

Here's the thing about an arrangement timeline: every element in it has an answer to the question *when*. That's its job. A clip at bar 9 is a statement that this thing happens ninth.

Which is great, later. It's poison early, because the *when* question forces itself in front of the *what* question. You start solving ordering problems — how do I get from the intro to the drop, what goes under the second verse — before you've established whether the drop is worth arriving at.

Session view removes time. Clips sit in a grid, launchable in any combination, looping until you stop them. Nothing has a position. You have a set of parts and no opinion yet about their sequence, and that ambiguity is not a limitation you're working around. It's the point.

@theory Musical ideas don't announce their function when you write them. That bass part you wrote thinking it was a verse might be the only thing that works under the final chorus, and you will never find that out if the first thing you do is drop it near the top of a timeline and build around it. Function is discovered by combination, not assigned by intention. Session view lets you audition combinations; a timeline makes you predict them.

## What session view is actually for

Not "loops." Loops are what people put in it. What it's *for* is answering three questions you cannot answer in a timeline without twenty minutes of dragging:

Does this bass work under those chords? Does it still work if the drums drop out? What happens if I run the chorus synth against the verse drums — is that a mistake, or is that the record?

You build a column per rough section, then you deliberately break the columns. Fire the drums from row 1 against the pad from row 4. Most combinations will be bad. Two will be interesting. One will be the thing you'd never have written on purpose, and that one is why you're here.

The producers who work this way tend to end up with more parts than they use. That's correct. You should be throwing away a third of what you make. A timeline discourages that, because deleting from a timeline leaves a hole you feel obligated to fill, whereas an unused clip in a grid is just an unused clip.

## The exact moment to switch

Here it is, and I'll defend it: **switch when you can play the whole song, live, from the grid, and you'd be happy if the recording of that performance was the final arrangement.**

Not "when I have enough parts." Not "when the loop sounds good." When you can *perform* it — fire the intro, bring in the drums, drop them out at the right moment, launch the chorus, land the ending — and the result holds up for two and a half minutes without you wincing.

If you can't perform it, you don't know the song yet, and moving to the timeline will only let you fake knowing it with copy-paste.

There's a second trigger, and it's a sound rather than a milestone: the moment you find yourself launching the *same* sequence of clips three times in a row without experimenting, you're done. Your hands have decided. That repetition is the arrangement telling you what it is. Go write it down.

@ear Set your clips going and play the song through once with your hands, no editing, no undo. Record nothing. Then do it again and pay attention only to where you got bored — not where it sounded bad, where your attention *left*. That's almost never where you thought it was. Fix that spot before you touch the timeline, because in the timeline it'll be buried under twelve other edits.

## What the arrangement view is for, since we're here

Committing. That's the entire list.

Once you're in the timeline, the questions change completely, and they should. Now it's: does the filter open over the right number of bars, does the vocal land two beats earlier than expected, is there a half-bar of silence before the last chorus. These are questions about *time*, and the timeline is the only place they can be asked properly.

The mistake in the other direction is real too. People fall in love with session view and never leave, and they end up with a beautiful grid of eleven clips and no finished music. The grid will happily let you tweak forever, because nothing in it ever has to be over. A track has to be over. Two hundred and forty thousand samples of it and then nothing — that's what finishing means, and only the timeline makes you face it.

@math A three-and-a-half-minute track at 120 BPM is 105 bars. At 140 it's 122. At 90, 78. Look at that number before you arrange — knowing you have roughly a hundred bars to fill changes how you budget a 16-bar intro, and it's a lot easier to plan a hundred than to plan "a song." Bars per minute is just BPM divided by beats per bar: 120/4 = 30.

## The move that ties it together

Perform the song from the grid and record that performance straight into the arrangement, in one pass. Then edit it.

This is different from building the arrangement clip by clip, and the difference is enormous. What lands on the timeline has the timing of a performance — slightly early launches, a section that ran two bars long because you liked it, a drop that happened when you felt it rather than where the grid said. Those accidents are the human part. You'd never program them.

Then you tighten it. Move a launch to the bar line if it needs it, leave it off if it doesn't, cut the section that ran long down to something honest. But you're editing a take now, not assembling a structure, and editing is a much easier problem than assembling.

I lost about four years to assembling. Every project sounded like it was built rather than played, and I blamed my sounds, my mixing, my monitors — everything except the fact that I'd been answering *when* before *what* for the entire time.

If you want to see how other people got from a grid of parts to a finished track, the [community](https://100lights.com/community) has published songs and projects you can open and dig through. Arrangements are much easier to learn by autopsy than by tutorial.

Stop driving. Go and play with the parts until one combination surprises you, and don't touch the timeline until your hands already know the song.
